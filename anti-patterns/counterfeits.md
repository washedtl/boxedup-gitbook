# Counterfeit traps when sourcing

🔴 **Critical.** Selling a counterfeit product, even unknowingly, destroys your account. Once Amazon flags you for counterfeit, the appeal path is brutal. This page shows where counterfeits hide and how to catch them before shipping to FBA.

## Why this matters

Counterfeit complaints are different from regular IP complaints:

| | Inauthentic complaint | Counterfeit complaint |
|---|---|---|
| Cause | Brand owner doesn't recognize seller | Brand has tested + confirmed fake |
| Severity | Medium | Severe |
| Reinstatement | 80% with POA | 30%–50%, even with evidence |
| Account-level impact | Warning | Often immediate suspension |
| Resolution time | 1–4 weeks | 1–6 months, sometimes never |

Counterfeit isn't a "you can fight it later" issue. It's an existential threat to your account.

## Where counterfeits hide

### Trap 1: Liquidation pallets

Liquidation pallets are the #1 source of counterfeits in BoxedUp's experience. Brand-protection teams often dump confirmed counterfeits into liquidation as "general merchandise."

**Mitigation:**
- Only buy pallets from major retailer-direct sources (Macy's, Walmart B-Stock, Target Direct)
- Avoid "general merchandise" wholesale lots from unknown sources
- Inspect every brand-name item against authentic samples
- Skip pallets with high-counterfeit-risk brands (Apple accessories, designer clothing)

### Trap 2: Online marketplace sourcing

Some sourcers buy from eBay, Mercari, Facebook Marketplace and resell on Amazon.

**Risk:** seller of the original might have been selling counterfeits unknowingly. You inherit the risk.

**Mitigation:** never source from individual-seller online marketplaces for high-counterfeit brands.

### Trap 3: "Wholesale" lots from non-distributor sellers

A "wholesale" listing on Alibaba, DHGate, or random websites offering brand-name items at 30% of retail. Almost always counterfeit.

**Mitigation:** only buy wholesale from documented authorized distributors. If you can't get an invoice from the brand directly, walk.

### Trap 4: Retail returns sold to outlet stores

Some retailers sell their returns to outlet stores at deep discount. If the original return was a counterfeit (customer returned it discovering it was fake), it ends up in outlet stores.

**Mitigation:** outlet sourcing carries elevated counterfeit risk. Inspect carefully.

### Trap 5: Brand discontinuations + gray-market resurfacing

Brand discontinues a product. Gray-market resellers buy remaining authorized stock. Some "remaining stock" was actually counterfeits made before the discontinuation. Very hard to detect.

**Mitigation:** for known discontinued items, source only from authorized channels with documented invoices.

### Trap 6: Price "too good to be true"

A standard signal: if a brand's MSRP is \$200 and a seller offers it for \$45, something's off. Either:
- Counterfeit
- Stolen goods
- Defective seconds being sold as authentic

**Mitigation:** sanity-check pricing. If it's 70%+ below MSRP and the source isn't a documented liquidation event, walk.

### Trap 7: Variation manipulation

A listing has "real" Apple lightning cables and counterfeit cables sharing the same ASIN (counterfeit-manipulated catalog entries). You source the listing, ship counterfeits unknowingly.

**Mitigation:** for high-counterfeit-risk products, photograph original packaging closely + verify authenticity markers.

## Authenticity verification by category

### Apparel
- ✅ Sewn-in labels with manufacturer info
- ✅ Quality stitching, no loose threads
- ✅ Genuine fabric weight + drape
- ✅ Branded packaging (boxes, polybags)
- ❌ Off-color logos
- ❌ Cheap fabric feel
- ❌ Misspellings on tags
- ❌ Generic packaging

### Electronics
- ✅ Genuine serial numbers (verify with brand)
- ✅ Build quality matches expectation
- ✅ Original box, manuals, accessories
- ❌ Mismatched serials
- ❌ Cheap-feeling materials
- ❌ Missing accessories
- ❌ Generic boxes

### Beauty / cosmetics
- ✅ Batch codes match brand's database
- ✅ Packaging quality matches authentic samples
- ✅ Smell + texture genuine
- ❌ Different consistency from authentic
- ❌ Different scent
- ❌ Slightly-off packaging colors

### Watches / accessories
- ✅ Weight matches authentic (usually heavier)
- ✅ Movement quality (smooth ticking, not jerky)
- ✅ Logo crispness
- ❌ Lighter than expected
- ❌ Inferior movement
- ❌ Off-color logos

### Toys
- ✅ Branded box with proper labeling
- ✅ Quality plastic / build
- ✅ Original tags + packaging
- ❌ Inferior plastic
- ❌ Misspelled brand names ("Lego" vs the real "LEGO")
- ❌ Generic packaging

### Trading cards / collectibles
- ✅ Card weight, texture, font
- ✅ UV reactivity (some authentics react to UV light)
- ✅ Holographic patterns match authentic
- ❌ Off colors, wrong fonts
- ❌ Wrong card stock thickness

## Authenticity tools

For high-stakes counterfeit prevention:

### Brand-specific tools
- **Apple**: Check the "Limited Warranty" portal — input serial, get authenticity confirmation
- **Beats by Dre**: serial-code lookup
- **Rolex / luxury watches**: certificates + serials checked against brand database
- **LEGO**: package weight, color codes, mold quality

### Generic tools
- **Manufacturer scan tools**: most large brands have "verify authenticity" portals
- **UV light**: some brands embed UV-reactive markers
- **Microscopic inspection**: counterfeit logos under high magnification show flaws

For high-counterfeit categories: invest in tools. \$50 in inspection equipment saves \$5,000 in inventory loss.

## Recovery if you sourced counterfeits

You bought 50 units, then realized they're fake.

### Step 1: STOP
Don't ship them to FBA. Don't list them. Mark them "DO NOT SELL."

### Step 2: Document
Photograph:
- Each unit
- Original packaging
- Brand labels
- Comparison to authentic (if available)
- Receipt of purchase

### Step 3: Return / refund
- Contact the supplier requesting refund
- For wholesale: cite "non-conforming goods"
- For retail: standard return process
- Pallet purchases: claim with the auction site within their disputes window

### Step 4: Don't sell elsewhere
You can't ethically sell counterfeits anywhere:
- eBay: same IP enforcement issues
- Facebook Marketplace: same
- Personal use: depends on local law

The right answer: dispose. Eat the loss. Lesson learned about sourcing channels.

### Step 5: Audit your sourcing
- Why did you source here?
- What signals did you miss?
- Add to "do not source" list

## What to do if you accidentally shipped counterfeit

You shipped 50 counterfeits to FBA before realizing.

### Step 1: Stop the bleeding
- Pause the listing immediately (Inventory → Edit listing → Status: Inactive)
- This prevents new sales while you resolve

### Step 2: Submit removal order
- Inventory → Manage Inventory → Action → Create Removal Order
- Choose "Disposal" (don't take counterfeits back home)
- Cost: ~\$0.30/unit + lost original cost

### Step 3: Refund any sold units proactively
- Pull order history for that ASIN
- Issue refunds + apology message
- Better to refund than wait for buyer complaints (that escalate to inauthentic claims)

### Step 4: Submit account audit
- Performance → Customer Service → "I want to proactively report this"
- Acknowledge the issue, your immediate corrective action
- Sometimes prevents future suspension when Amazon eventually files

### Step 5: Audit + improve
- Document the sourcing channel as "do not use"
- Improve prep workflow to catch authenticity issues before shipping
- Build invoice-path for high-risk brands

## Brand IP risk by category

Counterfeit risk levels by category:

| Category | Counterfeit risk | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Toys (mainstream brands) | Medium | LEGO, Disney, Nintendo top targets |
| Electronics accessories | High | Apple, Beats, premium phone gear |
| Designer apparel | Very high | Almost any luxury brand |
| Beauty (premium) | High | Olaplex, Drunk Elephant, etc. |
| Health supplements | Very high | OK to skip if you can |
| Trading cards | High | Pokémon especially |
| Watches | Very high | Avoid completely |
| Books | Low | Rare counterfeit issue |
| Generic CPG | Low | P&G, Unilever, etc. |
| Niche / private brands | Low | Lower visibility = lower risk |

## Brands you should never source from retail

Even with retail receipts, these are too risky:

- Apple (any product)
- Beats by Dre
- Sony (high-end)
- Bose
- Designer clothing (Chanel, Gucci, LV, Prada, etc.)
- Premium watches (Rolex, Omega, etc.)
- Premium fragrances (in restricted categories)

These have brand-protection teams that aggressively pursue resellers. Even authentic sourcing at retail can result in counterfeit complaints filed.

## Best practices summary

To avoid counterfeit traps:

1. ✅ **Source from authorized channels** — major retailers (Walmart, Target, etc.) or authorized distributors
2. ✅ **Save receipts** — proves authentic sourcing
3. ✅ **Inspect every unit** — don't ship without verification
4. ✅ **Skip high-counterfeit-risk brands** — don't source the brands listed above
5. ✅ **Use authentic samples** — keep one authentic unit at home for comparison
6. ✅ **Document everything** — photos, batch codes, serials
7. ✅ **Build brand registry / authorization** for any brand you source heavily
8. ❌ **Never source from unknown wholesale sites**
9. ❌ **Never source liquidation lots labeled "general merchandise"**
10. ❌ **Never trust "too good to be true" pricing**

## When you're falsely accused

Sometimes brands file counterfeit claims against authentic products. Your defense:

### Step 1: Don't panic
- Read the claim carefully
- Identify what evidence the brand provided

### Step 2: Submit invoice / receipt
- Provide your sourcing documentation
- Compare your unit's serial/batch to brand's database
- Submit photos showing authentic markers

### Step 3: Direct contact with brand
- Email the brand's IP team
- Provide your sourcing evidence
- Many brands will retract counterfeit claims if you have wholesale invoices

### Step 4: Escalate via Amazon
- Open additional case if needed
- Provide all evidence in single submission
- Be specific + factual

### Step 5: Last resort: lawyer up
- For serial false counterfeit accusations or significant inventory at risk
- Cost: \$500–\$5,000 for cease-and-desist letters
- Some brands back down when faced with legal pressure

## The counterfeit-prevention mindset

Treat every brand-name item you source as potentially counterfeit until proven otherwise.

This shifts from:
- "Why would this be fake?"

To:
- "How do I prove this is real?"

The proving process is what protects your account. Skip it = bet your account on every shipment.
