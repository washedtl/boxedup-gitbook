# The "too good to be true" checklist

🟡 → 🔴 — When a deal looks too good to be true, it usually is. This page is a tactical checklist of the warning signs that separate genuine opportunities from bait.

## The first principle

Markets are mostly efficient. Persistent obvious mispricings get arbitraged away within hours. If you see a "deal" that looks 5×–10× better than typical opportunities AND it's been available for >24 hours, ask: **why hasn't anyone else taken it?**

The answer is rarely "I'm the only one smart enough." It's usually "there's a hidden problem."

## The 12-point red-flag checklist

When a deal triggers your "too good" radar, run through these:

### Check 1: Is the listing suppressed?

Sometimes Amazon suppresses listings without removing them entirely. They look live but new sellers can't compete. Inventory you ship gets "stranded."

**How to check:** click the listing as a customer. If you can buy normally, it's live. If you see weird behaviors (no buy box, "currently unavailable" flickers, reviews disabled), suspect suppression.

### Check 2: Are reviews disabled?

Some categories (medical claims, etc.) have review restrictions. Listings with disabled reviews often have other restrictions you can't see.

**Action:** check reviews tab on the listing. If "no reviews allowed," extra caution.

### Check 3: Has Buy Box been suppressed recently?

Keepa shows when Buy Box was suppressed. Listings with frequent Buy Box suppressions = problematic.

**Action:** Keepa chart → look for missing pink Buy Box line in last 90 days. If gaps, expect more.

### Check 4: Is pricing manipulated by a single seller?

A solo seller holding a high Buy Box price = vulnerable. They often slash the moment competition appears.

**Action:** check seller-count history in Keepa. If it's been "1 seller" for months at high price, expect a price war on entry.

### Check 5: Is the brand "Brand Registered + actively enforcing"?

Listings that look profitable but the brand files complaints regularly = you're walking into an IP wall.

**Action:** check IP history (SAS shows recent complaint patterns). Avoid brands flagged red.

### Check 6: Is Amazon stockout-history sketchy?

A 90% Amazon-OOS listing might mean the listing is dead, not that demand is unmet.

**Action:** check 12-month chart. If Amazon was on then disappeared, look for the cause (recall? brand exclusivity change? listing suppression?).

### Check 7: Is the price too low at retail?

A \$30 retail item showing on Walmart for \$5 might be:
- Pricing error (Walmart will cancel your order)
- Damaged/expired stock
- Discontinued (no replenishability)
- Theft pattern (yes, retailers detect this)

**Action:** check retailer's website carefully. Read product details. Check related similar items' prices.

### Check 8: Are reviews suspicious?

A new listing with 200 reviews in 2 weeks = fake review activity. Amazon will eventually purge or suspend the listing.

**Action:** check review velocity in Keepa or by browsing reviews. If reviews look templated or suspiciously similar, the listing is at risk.

### Check 9: Is the FBA seller count too low?

Solo seller on a stable, popular ASIN = "why aren't others competing?" Possible reasons:
- Listing has hidden problems (gating, restrictions, IP risk)
- Low velocity disguised as high (BSR can mislead in subcategories)
- Recent suppression others are still sitting out

**Action:** investigate. If you can't find a clear reason, walk.

### Check 10: Is the variation listing tricking you?

Parent ASIN looks profitable; your scanned variation has different metrics.

**Action:** in Keepa, check the specific variation (size, color) you'd be selling. Verify per-variation BSR + Buy Box.

### Check 11: Is the offer history showing pile-on patterns?

3 → 12 sellers in 14 days = the deal got shared in deal-feed groups. Now it's cooked.

**Action:** check offer-count history. If recent pile-on, walk.

### Check 12: Does the math work after ALL fees?

Beginners forget:
- Inbound shipping
- Returns reserve
- Inbound placement fee
- Storage fees on slow movers

A "30% ROI" calc might actually be 15% after all costs. If 15% is below your floor, walk.

**Action:** use SAS or scanner with full fee model. Verify net profit, not gross.

## Specific "too good" patterns

### Pattern 1: The clearance avalanche
Walmart marks down 10 LEGO sets to \$5 each (normal \$30). Looks amazing.

**Reality:** likely:
- Pricing error → orders canceled
- Damaged returns → sold as "open box" (you can't list as new)
- Discontinued → no replen, future tank
- Theft mitigation → store wants the inventory off shelves

**Verification:** photograph the price tag in-store, photograph the receipt at checkout. If item arrives in same condition, you have at least 1 successful flip + receipt evidence.

### Pattern 2: The flash deal post

Discord member posts: "B0XXXXX, \$5 cost, \$45 sell, 200/mo velocity. GOLD."

**Reality:** by the time you click and try to buy:
- Stock may be gone
- Price tanked from pile-on
- Listing may have been suppressed
- Or it's actually a great deal — but you're #50 to act

**Verification:** independent verification before sourcing. Don't trust other people's math without your own.

### Pattern 3: The "exclusive" wholesale deal

Email: "I have wholesale inventory of [brand]. \$12/unit. Sells \$45 retail. Reach out for opportunities."

**Reality:** probably:
- Counterfeit
- Stolen
- Diverted (gray-market product brand will complain about)
- Scam — they'll take payment and disappear

**Verification:** require:
- Manufacturer-direct invoice
- Letter of authorization from brand
- Proof of business existence (LLC, EIN, banking history)
- References from other resellers

If they refuse documentation: walk.

### Pattern 4: The Discord-shared "secret" supplier

A deep-tier member shares "their" supplier in private DMs. Promises high margins.

**Reality:** could be legitimate, but more often:
- Saturated already (everyone they shared with is now competing)
- Counterfeit middleman
- Multi-level scheme where you're the next tier of buyer

**Verification:** test order at small scale. Inspect units carefully. Verify with brand directly if possible.

### Pattern 5: The "guaranteed flip" course

\$2,000 course promising "\$10,000/month within 90 days" with proven flips taught.

**Reality:**
- The flips taught in courses are usually saturated by the time you learn them (everyone else who took the course also tried them)
- Course promises rarely match reality
- Your money is better spent on inventory + tools

**Verification:** check course reviews from neutral sources. Most "Amazon FBA gurus" are selling courses to escape failed reselling.

## When the "too good" really IS just good

Sometimes deals are real:

### Real pattern 1: Restock/Monitor catches
You set up Tempo on an OOS item. It restocks at original retail. You buy at low cost while market still elevated.

**Why it's real:** information asymmetry. Most resellers don't have monitors. The market is genuinely inefficient at this specific moment.

### Real pattern 2: A2A pricing anomalies
Amazon's algorithm misprices an item 50%+ below historical. Window: hours.

**Why it's real:** Amazon's automation is imperfect. Mean reversion is real.

### Real pattern 3: New replens you discover
You do a KPF query nobody else has run. You find an item with stable demand + low competition.

**Why it's real:** information advantage from systematic discovery (KPF skill matters here).

### Real pattern 4: Wholesale relationships you build
You secure a wholesale agreement with a small brand. Your competition is limited to a handful of authorized resellers.

**Why it's real:** relationship moats. Hard to replicate quickly.

The pattern: real opportunities have **structural reasons** you can articulate. "Too good to be true" deals can't be explained — that's why they're suspect.

## The 5-minute reality check

Before sourcing any deal, run this:

```
1. Is the math actually correct? (re-verify all fees + costs)
2. Why does this opportunity exist? (articulate a structural reason)
3. Has the listing been stable for 90+ days? (Keepa check)
4. What's the worst-case scenario if I'm wrong? (loss tolerance)
5. Is my "edge" plausible? (information, monitoring, relationship)
```

If you can't answer all 5 confidently, walk.

## When to trust your gut

After 200+ flips, your instincts develop. When something feels off but you can't articulate why:

- It's pattern recognition firing below conscious thought
- You've seen something similar fail before
- Trust the gut even when math looks fine

Walking from a "good" deal that triggered a gut warning is rarely costly. Sourcing despite the gut warning often is.

## Recovery from a bad source

You sourced a "too good" deal, lost money. Now what?

### Step 1: Stop the bleeding
- Don't reorder
- Don't try to "ride it out"
- Liquidate inventory at whatever price moves it

### Step 2: Document the lesson
- What signals did you miss?
- What rules will prevent recurrence?
- Add to your "do not source" list

### Step 3: Move on
- Don't dwell
- Don't post recriminations
- The cost is already sunk; minimize future cost

The mark of a pro: failures get filed as data, not trauma.

## A final thought

Resellers who succeed long-term aren't the ones who land every deal — they're the ones who avoid the bait.

Your sourcing competition is full of beginners chasing every "great deal." They lose money slowly. The pros pass on 90%+ of opportunities and source only the ones that survive scrutiny.

Be the second type. Walk often. Source rarely.
