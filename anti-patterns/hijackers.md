# Hijackers and how to fight back

🟡 → 🔴 — A "hijacker" is a seller who lists on a Brand Registered listing without authorization. The term is most relevant if you do private label — but understanding hijackers is also useful for arbitrage sellers who frequently get accused of being one.

## Two perspectives on this page

This page covers both:
- **You're the brand owner** — someone is hijacking your listing → how to fight back
- **You're the arbitrage seller** — a brand owner thinks you're a hijacker → how to defend yourself

Both are common in the BoxedUp community.

## What a hijacker is (and isn't)

### True hijackers
- Listing on a Brand Registered ASIN without authorization
- Often selling counterfeit/fake products under the legitimate brand's listing
- Sometimes listing real product but obtained illegally (theft, gray market)
- Goal: piggyback on the established listing's traffic for quick sales

### NOT hijackers (but often confused)
- **Authorized resellers** — they bought the product legally and are reselling. **This is legal under the First Sale Doctrine in the US.**
- **RA/OA arbitrage sellers** — same thing. Legally bought retail product, resold on Amazon.

The distinction matters because brand owners often **call all third-party sellers "hijackers"** when they actually mean "people we don't want competing on our listing."

## If you're the brand owner

You have your own listing, Brand Registered, and someone else is selling on it. Three escalation paths:

### Step 1: Contact the seller
- Open the listing → click on the seller's name → "Ask a question"
- Polite request: "Hi, we noticed you're selling on our brand's listing. Could you provide proof of authorization or remove your offer?"
- Many casual sellers will remove on request — they may not have realized it was Brand Registered

### Step 2: Test buy
If the seller won't remove and you suspect counterfeit:
- Buy 1 unit from them yourself
- Compare against your authentic product (packaging, smell, weight, materials, batch codes)
- If counterfeit confirmed: photograph everything carefully

### Step 3: File via Brand Registry
- Brand Registry portal → Report a violation → Suspected counterfeit / IP infringement
- Submit:
  - The seller's account
  - Order ID of your test buy
  - Side-by-side photos of authentic vs hijacker product
  - Your batch/serial code records (showing the hijacker's batch isn't from your manufacturing)
- Amazon typically removes the hijacker's offer in 24–72 hours

### Step 4: Test buy via Transparency
If you've enrolled your brand in **Amazon Transparency** (codes-on-products program), every authentic unit has a unique scannable code. Hijackers can't replicate codes. Filing becomes a 1-click process.

Transparency is the gold standard for brand defense. Costs ~\$0.01–\$0.05 per code, requires manufacturer integration. Worth it for any brand doing >\$50K/month.

## If you're the arbitrage seller (most BoxedUp readers)

A brand has filed against you claiming you're hijacking. You're not — you legally bought retail product. Defense path:

### Don't panic
The complaint is automated. Amazon's first response is to suppress your offer. You have 17 days to respond before the complaint becomes a strike on your account.

### Gather evidence
You need:
- **Receipt** — the original purchase from a retail store (Target, Walmart, etc.)
- **Photo of receipt** with the date visible
- **Photo of the unit** showing condition matches new (not used)
- **Order number from your sourcing** (if OA)
- Ideally: a wholesale invoice from an authorized distributor

### File your appeal

**Plan of Action template:**

```
Subject: Appeal of inauthentic complaint on ASIN [ASIN]

Dear Amazon team,

I am writing to appeal the inauthentic complaint filed on ASIN [ASIN]
on [date]. I believe this complaint is in error.

Root cause:
- I sourced this product legally from [retailer name] on [date]
- I have attached the receipt as evidence
- The unit shipped to FBA was new, factory-sealed, and identical to
  the authentic product

Corrective action:
- I have suspended further FBA shipments of this ASIN pending review
- I am committed to sourcing only from authorized channels going forward

Preventive action:
- I will obtain wholesale invoices from authorized distributors before
  re-listing this ASIN
- I am implementing a sourcing audit process to prevent similar
  complaints in the future

Please find attached:
- Receipt from [retailer] dated [date]
- Photo of authentic product
- [Other relevant evidence]

Thank you for your consideration,
[Your name]
[Your seller account email]
```

### Submit and wait
- Account Health → Suspended ASINs → Reinstate
- Submit your POA + evidence
- Amazon's response time: 1–14 days
- Most appeals win on first submission with good evidence

### If denied
- Re-submit with stronger evidence
- Reach out directly to the brand owner — sometimes they'll retract a complaint if you have legitimate sourcing
- If still stuck, hire a Plan of Action specialist (\$200–\$1500 for high-stakes accounts)

## Preventive measures (arbitrage sellers)

To avoid hijacker-related complaints:

1. **Avoid Brand Registered listings without authorized invoices**
   - The "Storefront" badge on a listing = Brand Registered = high IP-claim risk
   - Stick to listings without storefront for RA flips

2. **Save every receipt for 18+ months**
   - Photo each receipt at purchase
   - Cloud-backed folder by month
   - Cross-reference to FBA shipment IDs

3. **Build wholesale relationships for top SKUs**
   - For your top 5–10 replens, get a wholesale invoice path
   - Then RA receipts become a backup, not your only defense

4. **Don't ignore "stranded inventory" notifications**
   - When a listing gets suppressed, your remaining inventory becomes stranded
   - Submit removal orders immediately to avoid LTSF on suppressed inventory

## The reality check for arbitrage sellers

Brand-vs-arbitrage tension is real. Your legal position:

✅ **First Sale Doctrine** — In the US, you can resell a legitimately purchased product. Brands cannot prevent this in court.

❌ **Amazon's TOS** — Amazon can let brands enforce gating regardless of legal rights. Amazon is a private platform; they can ban anyone for any reason.

This means you can be **legally right** and still **lose your account.**

Adapt accordingly:
- Avoid brands known to file aggressively (see [IP complaints](ip-complaints.md))
- Build wholesale paths for high-value SKUs
- Source from gray-area brands with caution
- When in doubt, ask in `#sourcing-help` before sourcing

## When to walk away

If a brand has filed multiple complaints against you, even unsuccessfully, **stop sourcing that brand.** Amazon's automation accumulates complaint history. After enough complaints (~3–5), Amazon may:
- Suspend the entire brand from your account
- Hold you to a higher evidence bar for future complaints
- Risk-flag your account for related categories

Some brands aren't worth the fight. Move on.
