# IP complaints and how to avoid them

🔴 **Critical.** IP (Intellectual Property) complaints are the #1 reason BoxedUp resellers lose accounts. Read this twice.

## What an IP complaint is

A brand owner files a complaint with Amazon claiming you're infringing their **trademark, copyright, patent, or design** by selling their product. Amazon's response is automated and brutal:

1. Your listing for that ASIN gets **immediately suppressed**
2. The complaint shows up in your **Account Health → Policy Compliance**
3. Multiple complaints in a short window = **account suspension**
4. Some complaints (especially design patents) can take **months** to resolve

⚠️ **You don't have to be guilty.** Brand owners can and do file frivolous complaints — sometimes intentionally to clear competition off their listings. Amazon doesn't investigate. They suppress first, ask questions later (if at all).

## The four types of IP complaints

| Type | What it claims | How common | How dangerous |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Trademark** | You're using their brand name without authorization | Very common | Resolvable with invoice |
| **Copyright** | You're using their images/text in your listing | Moderate | Easy fix (remove content) |
| **Design patent** | The physical product design is patented | Rare but devastating | Almost unwinnable |
| **Utility patent** | The product's functionality is patented | Rare | Resolvable but slow |

## The brands most likely to file

The BoxedUp Discord's "stay away" list, by complaint frequency:

### Tier 1 — file complaints aggressively, almost no winnable defense

- **Disney** (and all Disney-owned: Marvel, Star Wars, Pixar)
- **Nintendo** (especially anything Mario, Zelda, Pokémon, Switch)
- **LEGO** (without LEGO ungating, every set is risk)
- **Pokémon** (TCG cards, plush, anything)
- **Apple** (accessories, AirPods, anything)
- **Adidas, Nike, Under Armour** (counterfeit-paranoid)
- **Beats by Dre**

### Tier 2 — file occasionally, winnable with proper invoice

- **Sony, Bose** (electronics)
- **Dyson** (vacuums, hair tools)
- **Stanley** (cups — yes, the cups)
- **Hydro Flask, Yeti**
- **Olaplex, Drunk Elephant** (beauty majors)
- **L'Oreal** (most of their portfolio)

### Tier 3 — generally safe with retail receipts, but watch the listing

- Most CPG brands (Pepsi, P&G, Unilever) — they file rarely
- Most toy brands not on Tier 1
- Generic / private-label brands

> **The BoxedUp rule:** if it's Tier 1, **don't source it from RA receipts** unless you have wholesale invoices. The retail receipt won't save you when Disney files.

## The five ways to NOT get an IP complaint

### 1. Source from authorized channels

The cleanest defense is a **wholesale invoice from an authorized distributor** of that brand. RA receipts are legally fine but Amazon doesn't always accept them — especially for Tier 1 brands.

**Authorized = direct from brand, or distributor with documented brand authorization.**

### 2. Don't sell on listings where the brand is "Brand Registered"

Brand Registered listings show a small badge in the top-right of the listing page. Brand Registered brands get **fast-track removal of unauthorized sellers** — Amazon will side with them by default.

**Check before you list:**
- Does the brand have its own Storefront? (Usually = Brand Registry)
- Is the listing fully optimized with A+ content, video, lots of high-res images? (Same)
- Does the listing description say "Sold by [Brand]" or "Authorized retailer of [Brand]"? (Major red flag)

### 3. Match the listing exactly

Many trademark complaints come from **listing variations**. If you ship a unit that's:
- A different SKU than the listing's UPC/EAN
- A different size, color, or count than what's listed
- Repackaged differently

…the brand can claim "inauthentic" or trademark misuse.

**Always match the listing to the unit. If they don't match, find a different listing or skip it.**

### 4. Avoid clearance/closeout for major brands

When Disney clears out a Frozen plush at Target, those leftover units flooding Amazon is exactly what Disney's IP team scans for. Even if you have the receipt, you're a target.

**The pattern:** big brand + big clearance + lots of FBA sellers piling in = IP wave incoming. **Get out before the wave.**

### 5. Keep records like your account depends on it (because it does)

For every flip:

- Receipt photo (geo-tagged if possible)
- ASIN you listed against
- Date sourced, date listed, date first sale
- Any wholesale invoices

Store these in a **structured folder per month**. When a complaint comes, you have 17 days to respond — you don't want to be hunting for a receipt while your account bleeds.

## What happens when you get one

The 24-hour playbook:

### Hour 0: You get the email

"Notice of intellectual property infringement" → from `notice-dispute@amazon.com` or directly from a brand's IP firm.

**Do not panic. Do not delete the listing yourself. Do not respond emotionally.**

### Hours 0–4: Read the complaint carefully

The notice will say:
- Which ASIN
- Which type of IP claim (trademark / copyright / patent)
- Sometimes the complainant's contact info
- A "complaint ID"

Screenshot everything. Save the email.

### Hours 4–24: Decide your path

**Path A: Remove inventory & move on** (most common for low-stakes complaints)
- Submit a removal order in FBA
- Acknowledge the complaint in Account Health
- Don't sell on that listing again
- Cost: \$0 + lost inventory value

**Path B: Reach out to complainant directly**
- Email the brand's IP team (often listed in the complaint)
- Provide your sourcing invoices
- Politely ask for retraction
- Many will retract if you have legit invoices and aren't a counterfeiter
- Cost: 1–2 weeks of waiting; sometimes free, sometimes a brand asks you to "destroy remaining inventory"

**Path C: File a counter-notice with Amazon**
- Only if you're 100% sure the complaint is wrong
- Risks: Amazon sides with the brand by default
- Cost: medium effort, sometimes accelerates resolution

**Path D: Lawyer up**
- For serial complaints from the same brand or a 6-figure inventory exposure
- Cost: \$300–\$1,500 retainer, but worth it for big positions

## When complaints stack — the suspension threshold

Amazon doesn't publish a hard number, but the BoxedUp consensus:

- **1 complaint** = ignored if resolved quickly
- **2–3 complaints in 90 days** = warning, possible suspension
- **4+ complaints in 90 days** = near-certain suspension
- **A pattern of complaints in one brand category** = brand-level ban (you can't sell that brand again, ever)

If you see **2 complaints stack within a month**, stop sourcing new inventory in any related category until both are resolved. Don't pile on a third.

## The brand-registry brand graveyard

Some brands have made it their full-time job to clear Amazon of unauthorized resellers. Even with retail receipts, you will lose. The current "do not source" list:

- Apple (any accessory)
- Disney (any character)
- Nintendo (any product)
- LEGO without LEGO ungating
- Pokemon (any product)
- Stanley (the cups)
- Chanel, Gucci, LV, any luxury
- Most pharmaceuticals, even OTC
- Most baby formula and infant products

## A note on the "private label" myth

Some sellers say "I'll just sell my own brand to avoid IP." Two truths:

1. **Yes**, owning your brand and being Brand Registered shields you from inbound complaints.
2. **No**, it doesn't shield you from people claiming patent infringement on the *product itself* (especially if you sourced from Alibaba and someone else patented the design in the US).

Private label is a different game with different rules — see [Private Label](../sourcing/private-label.md). Don't switch to PL just to escape IP complaints.
