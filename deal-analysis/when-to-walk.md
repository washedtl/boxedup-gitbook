# When to walk away

🟡 **Intermediate.** The most profitable decision in arbitrage is often "no." Beginners chase every deal. Pros pass on 90%+ of what they scan. Knowing when to walk is the difference between a profitable operation and a slowly bleeding one.

## The high-confidence walks

These are unambiguous "no" signals. If any single one is true, walk:

### Walk 1: Amazon currently selling
Orange line in Keepa = Amazon present. They win Buy Box ~95% of the time. Your inventory will sit.

**Exception:** Amazon has been in/out for 90+ days with consistent gaps. You can play the gaps if you're set up to monitor. Risky for beginners.

### Walk 2: Tier 1 brand without authorization
Disney, Nintendo, Apple, LEGO, Pokemon, Stanley. Even with retail receipts, the IP risk isn't worth it.

**Exception:** wholesale invoice from authorized distributor. Otherwise, walk.

### Walk 3: Listing suppressed or pending review
Listing shows error message, no Buy Box, or "Currently Unavailable." Don't source — listing health is broken.

### Walk 4: Account-level restriction
Your scanner shows "Cannot list this product." Account flagged for this category/brand. No appeal path. Walk.

### Walk 5: Hazmat without prior approval
Battery, aerosol, flammable items not in your approved list. Hazmat approval takes 1–6 weeks. Walk for now.

### Walk 6: Buy Box price below your cost
You'd be selling at a loss. Doesn't matter how good the listing looks otherwise. Walk.

### Walk 7: 15+ FBA sellers competing
Listing is cooked. Buy Box rotation gives you 1/15 share at best. Margins likely race-to-bottom. Walk.

### Walk 8: New ASIN (<90 days)
Velocity data unreliable. Could be a hit or a dud. Wait 90 days and reassess.

## The judgment calls

These are softer signals. One alone might not be enough; combinations should make you walk:

### Soft signal 1: ROI < 30% (RA) or 20% (OA)
Your floor varies by sourcing channel. If a deal's below your floor, look for another.

**Override:** if velocity is exceptionally high (50+ sales/month for you) and storage cost is low, lower ROI floor might still work. Run the math.

### Soft signal 2: BSR drops < 6 in 30 days
Slow seller. Will sit at FBA accumulating storage fees.

**Override:** if profit/unit is \$15+, slow movement is okay (you're playing margin, not velocity).

### Soft signal 3: Buy Box declining trend
30-day average lower than 90-day. Sellers cutting prices to clear inventory.

**Override:** if decline is small (<5%) and sellers seem to have stabilized, possibly okay.

### Soft signal 4: Brand IP risk yellow
Brand registered but no recent complaint history. Some risk but workable.

**Override:** combined with low FBA seller count + high margin, can be acceptable. Never combine with low margin.

### Soft signal 5: Recent offer-count spike
3 → 12 sellers in 30 days. Pile-on signal.

**Override:** if you're early in the spike (your scan IS the early sign), you might still profit. Hard to time.

### Soft signal 6: Single seller currently
You'd be entering a listing nobody else is on. Could be:
- Untapped opportunity (good)
- Listing has hidden problem (suppressed history, IP issue, gating that doesn't show)

**Test:** investigate why nobody else is selling. If you can't find the reason, that IS the reason — walk.

### Soft signal 7: Variation listing without your specific variation
Your scanned UPC matches a child variation that has weak velocity, even if parent looks great.

**Override:** if your variation specifically has decent metrics, source. Don't be misled by parent.

## The "deal feels off" instinct

After 100+ sourcing decisions, you'll get a gut feel. When something seems off but you can't articulate why, **trust the gut**.

Common patterns that produce the "off" feeling:
- Too good to be true (price way better than market)
- Brand seems wrong (off-brand spelling, weird packaging)
- Listing has been recently edited (changes sometimes mask issues)
- Reviews seem fake (sudden spike in 5-star reviews from "Amazon Customer")
- Multiple similar listings exist (catalog duplication issues)

You can't fully verify these. The "off" feeling is your pattern recognition firing. Walk.

## Walking after committing inventory

What if you've already shipped the inventory and now realize it's a bad call?

### Option 1: Drop price aggressively
Cut 10–20% from current Buy Box. Try to sell through fast.

### Option 2: Submit removal order
Get inventory back. Sell elsewhere (eBay, Facebook Marketplace) or hold for next opportunity.

### Option 3: Disposal
For low-value inventory, disposing (~\$0.30/unit) is cheaper than continued FBA fees.

### Option 4: Hold and pray
Hope the situation reverses. Often the worst choice — fees compound while you wait.

The right choice depends on:
- Your inventory value (high-value: removal; low-value: disposal)
- The reason for walking (price tank: drop price; IP claim: removal/disposal)
- Your IPI status (low IPI: aggressive disposal to clean up)

## When to walk from a SUPPLIER, not a SKU

Some signals point to leaving the entire source, not just one SKU:

### Supplier signal 1: Repeated quality issues
Three consecutive shipments had damaged units. The supplier has process problems.

### Supplier signal 2: Delayed shipments
Expected delivery in 5 days, actual 14 days. You missed Buy Box momentum each time.

### Supplier signal 3: Inventory inaccuracies
Ordered 100, received 87. Supplier shorts you regularly.

### Supplier signal 4: MAP enforcement weakening
Your wholesale brand has 30 sellers undercutting each other. Brand isn't enforcing its own MAP.

### Supplier signal 5: Counterfeit risk
Suspect units in shipments — manufacturer changed sources or sourcing got compromised.

When 2+ of these happen, walk from the supplier entirely. Repeat customers of bad suppliers waste capital + time.

## When to walk from CATEGORY

Sometimes an entire category becomes unworkable:

### Category collapse signal 1: Mass IP enforcement
Beauty, Apparel, Electronics — these categories have intermittent waves of IP enforcement that hurt arbitrage sellers. When you see the wave coming (members getting suspended), walk before you're caught.

### Category collapse signal 2: Saturation across the board
Toys & Games during Q4 sometimes sees 100+ sellers on every popular SKU. Margins collapse.

### Category collapse signal 3: Amazon vertical takeover
Amazon launches "Amazon Basics" + "Amazon Brand" lines in your category. They start dominating Buy Box.

### Category collapse signal 4: Regulatory shift
Beauty + Topicals have had FDA-driven listing requirements changes. If your inventory becomes non-compliant, walk.

## When to walk from RESELLING entirely

The hardest decision. Some triggers:

- ❌ **Six months of net losses** despite reasonable effort
- ❌ **Account suspended without reinstatement path**
- ❌ **Capital fully tied up in slow-moving inventory** with no recovery in sight
- ❌ **Mental health degrading** from the stress
- ❌ **Better opportunity available** (different business, employment, etc.)

Reselling isn't for everyone. It's a real business with real risk and real grind. If 6+ months of your best effort doesn't yield positive momentum, the math is telling you something. Walking from the entire game is sometimes the right call.

## How to walk gracefully

When you walk from a deal, supplier, or category:

### Don't dwell
Don't relitigate the decision for weeks. Make the call, move on.

### Document the lesson
Note in your records WHY you walked. Future-you reading old notes is the best mentor.

### Free up the capital
If you walked from inventory, get rid of it (drop price / removal / disposal). Hoarded inventory is dead capital.

### Watch for re-entry
Sometimes a walked-from SKU becomes attractive again 6 months later. The signal that prompted walking has resolved. Re-evaluate.

## The scoreboard

A useful self-check: track your walk vs source ratio.

- **Beginner:** sources 50%, walks 50%. Too many bad sources slip through.
- **Intermediate:** sources 25%, walks 75%. Filter is improving.
- **Pro:** sources 5–10%, walks 90%+. Filter is sharp.

The pro doesn't see fewer opportunities. They see more — but reject more. Counterintuitively, the more you walk, the more you profit.

## Hardest walks

Three psychologically hard walks:

### Hard walk 1: A deal you'd love to source
Profit margin huge. But ONE signal says walk. Beginners override. Pros walk.

The signal you can't ignore: **gating you don't have**, **Tier 1 brand without invoice**, **Amazon currently selling**.

### Hard walk 2: A friend's deal recommendation
A Discord member surfaces a deal. You don't see why it works for you. Beginners source out of social pressure. Pros decline politely and walk.

Their math isn't your math. Different cost basis, different account history, different risk profile.

### Hard walk 3: A previously-winning replen
Your replen of 6 months has decayed. New competitors, falling Buy Box. You want to "wait it out." Pros pull the plug at the first 30-day decline.

Sentimental attachment to past performance is poison. Walk.
