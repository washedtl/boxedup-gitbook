# Seasonal vs evergreen ASINs

🟡 **Intermediate.** Most resellers focus only on what's selling RIGHT NOW. Pros also track what'll sell in 90 days, 6 months, or next year. Mastering seasonality unlocks margin patterns that aren't available to short-term thinkers.

## Seasonal vs evergreen

### Evergreen
Constant demand year-round. Examples:
- Pet food
- Kitchen utensils
- Cleaning supplies
- Most office supplies
- Generic toys (LEGO, Hot Wheels)

Sales chart is roughly flat over 12 months. Predictable, stable, lower margin.

### Seasonal
Demand spikes and dies on a calendar pattern. Examples:
- Christmas decor (Q4 spike)
- Halloween costumes (October)
- Pool/beach gear (summer)
- Back-to-school supplies (August)
- Allergy medications (spring)

Sales chart has tall spikes + long flats. Big margin upside, requires patient capital.

### Hybrid
Some demand year-round, but spikes seasonally. Examples:
- Coffee gear (gift season + summer iced)
- Camping equipment (spring + summer peak)
- Vitamins (January spike + steady rest of year)

Lower volatility than pure seasonal, more upside than pure evergreen.

## How to identify seasonality

Three checks on Keepa:

### Check 1: 12-month chart
Set Keepa to 1-year view. Look for:
- **Repeated spikes** at same time each year = seasonal
- **Flat with minor variance** = evergreen
- **Single past spike never repeated** = trend-driven, not seasonal

### Check 2: BSR drops by month
If December had 500 drops and June had 5, that's seasonal.

### Check 3: Search trends
Use Google Trends to check the product term:
- Christmas + spike in November = seasonal
- Term used year-round = evergreen

## The seasonal opportunity

Why seasonal items have unique margin:

### Pre-peak buying
Buy in March when demand is dead. Hold through summer/fall. Sell in November at 3× margin.

A Christmas decor item bought at \$8 retail in March could sell for \$35 in November. Even with 8 months of FBA storage, you might net \$15/unit profit instead of \$3 in May.

### Post-peak buying
Buy in January when retailers liquidate seasonal stock at 70% off. Hold for 10 months. Sell next October.

January Christmas decor at \$3 → November \$25 (well-timed) = enormous margin.

### Out-of-season arbitrage
A summer-themed kitchen item sells year-round but spikes in summer. Buy off-peak from clearance, sell on-peak at premium.

## Storage cost considerations

Seasonal plays require math on storage:

```
Net per-unit profit = 
  (Sell price) - (Buy cost) - (Amazon fees) - (Storage × months)
```

Storage cost example:
- Standard-size unit at 0.014 cuft
- Off-peak rate: \$0.87/cuft/month = \$0.012/unit/month
- 8-month hold (March → November): \$0.10 in storage
- Q4 peak rate (Oct, Nov, Dec): 3× = \$0.036/unit/month
- 3 months of Q4 storage: \$0.11

Total 8-month storage cost: ~\$0.21/unit

For high-margin seasonal plays, \$0.21 is negligible. For low-margin, it can eat 30%+ of profit.

⚠️ **Avoid the LTSF cliff.** If you hold into the next year (>365 days), the LTSF surcharge of \$0.15/unit minimum hits. Plan to sell through within 12 months.

## Sourcing strategies by season

### Spring (Mar-May): liquidate Q4 + source summer
- Watch for Q4 leftovers (Christmas, holiday) at retailer clearance
- Begin sourcing summer/outdoor for May–July sales
- Allergy season opportunity (April–June)

### Summer (Jun-Aug): source fall/winter
- Walmart/Target start showing fall items in late July
- Halloween costumes appear August
- Christmas decor sometimes shelved by August

### Fall (Sep-Nov): source Q4 + source spring
- Big window for Q4 sourcing (Sep–Oct)
- Some retailers start Q1 (post-Christmas) clearance early November

### Winter (Dec-Feb): minimum sourcing, max sell
- Don't ship new inventory in December (receive too slow)
- Sell through Q4 inventory
- January: aggressive removals for IPI, January clearance sourcing

## Specific seasonal arbitrage plays

### Christmas decor → next year
Buy in January at 70% off. Hold. Sell next October–November at 3× margin.

Risks:
- Trend can shift (specific decor styles fall out)
- Storage cost over 10 months
- LTSF if you miss the window

Reward: 100%+ ROI for patient capital.

### Halloween costumes → next year
Same pattern. Less risk than Christmas (costumes don't go out of style as fast). November buy, next October sell.

### Summer outdoor → next year
Pool toys, beach items at end-of-summer clearance. Hold until next May–June.

### Gift sets → next holiday
Mother's Day gift sets at 60% off in June → November Christmas gift season.

### Halloween → Christmas
A surprising play: certain Halloween items (e.g., generic spooky decor) sell year-round to "horror" enthusiasts. Buy November, sell continuously.

## Q4 strategy for arbitrage

Q4 is the highest-volume period for FBA. For BoxedUp:

### August preparation
- Build inventory pipeline
- Verify ungating for any Q4 categories
- Sourcing pace: 1.5× normal

### September execution
- Source Q4 inventory aggressively
- Ship to FBA by mid-October to ensure reception by Black Friday

### October sourcing
- Last call for Black Friday inventory
- Replenishment of hot SKUs from earlier shipments
- Watch for "Toy Q4 ungating" notifications if they apply to you

### November
- Limited new sourcing (receive times balloon)
- Manage what's at FBA
- Adjust pricing for Black Friday (often raise — not lower)

### December
- Almost no sourcing
- Maximum selling
- Plan January cleanup

### January
- Removals + dispositions for slow Q4 movers
- Sourcing post-holiday clearance
- IPI cleanup

## When seasonality fails

Common seasonal-arb failures:

### Failure 1: Trend-driven season
You sourced "Disney Frozen ornaments" expecting a Christmas spike. Frozen sales cooled by Q4. Demand never came.

**Fix:** stick to evergreen seasonal items (generic Christmas decor, classic Halloween) over trend-driven (specific TV shows, movie tie-ins).

### Failure 2: Storage exceeds margin
You bought 200 units in March, sell-through is slow, by December you've eaten \$3/unit in storage on \$2/unit margin items.

**Fix:** seasonality plays only on items with \$5+ margin to absorb storage.

### Failure 3: Missed peak window
You shipped December 1, expecting Christmas sales. Receive took 3 weeks. Inventory live December 22 — too late.

**Fix:** ship Q4 inventory by November 15 minimum. Better mid-October.

### Failure 4: Listing got merged / changed
Your seasonal item's listing got combined with another. Inventory stranded.

**Fix:** monitor listing health weekly during seasonal periods.

## When evergreen wins

Evergreen products win in:

### Predictable cash flow
Same revenue every month, no seasonal swings.

### Lower storage cost ratio
Items sell in 30–60 days, never accumulate storage debt.

### Easier capital planning
You know exactly how much capital is tied up at any time.

### IPI stability
Faster turnover = better sell-through = healthier IPI.

For most BoxedUp members, **70% evergreen + 30% seasonal** is the right mix. Evergreen funds the operation; seasonal goosed margins.

## Tracking seasonal patterns

Add a "seasonality tag" to your Found Profit List:
- 🌸 **Spring** (March–May)
- ☀️ **Summer** (June–August)
- 🍂 **Fall** (September–November)
- ❄️ **Winter** (December–February)
- 🟢 **Evergreen** (year-round)

Quarterly review: which seasonal categories actually delivered? Which evergreens have steady demand?

After 12 months of tracking, you'll have a pattern map specific to your sourcing — better than any generic seasonal guide.
