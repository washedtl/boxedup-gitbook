# Multi-pack & bundle rules

🟡 **Intermediate.** Bundles and multi-packs let you create new ASINs from existing components. Done right, you avoid Buy Box competition entirely. Done wrong, Amazon rejects your listing or merges it into someone else's.

## The two product types

### Multi-pack
Same product, sold in a quantity. Examples:
- 6-pack of toothpaste
- 4-pack of LEGO sets
- 3-pack of socks

The brand often has its own multi-pack listing. You're listing under that ASIN.

### Bundle
Different products sold together as one unit. Examples:
- A coffee maker + 2 lbs of beans + a mug
- A baby bottle + sterilizer + drying rack
- A skincare set with cleanser, toner, moisturizer

Bundles must follow Amazon's bundle rules. Done correctly, you create your own ASIN that no one else competes on.

## Why bundles are interesting

For arbitrage sellers, bundles offer:
- **No Buy Box competition** — your ASIN, your offer
- **Higher perceived value** — buyers see the bundle as more than the sum
- **Brand-protection sidestep** — bundles aren't typically Brand Registered
- **Margin upside** — you can price 20–40% above component sum

Trade-off: more work to set up, more unsold-inventory risk, and lower velocity per ASIN than a hot single-ASIN listing.

## Amazon's bundle rules

To list a bundle, it must:

1. **Cohere** — products must work together for a unified purpose. "Random items in a box" isn't a bundle.

2. **Have ONE primary product** — the most expensive or central item drives the listing's category.

3. **Include 2–5 components** — single component = not a bundle; 6+ components = "lot" (different rules).

4. **Sell as a single, sealed unit** — physically packaged together so a customer can't disassemble before purchase.

5. **Have a unique title** — must include "Bundle" in the title. Example: "Coffee Lovers Bundle: French Press, Premium Beans, & Mug."

6. **Have ONE owner** — only the bundle creator can sell on the listing (mostly enforced via brand controls).

7. **Be in an allowed category** — most categories permit bundles; some don't (e.g., grocery has restrictions on perishable bundles).

## How to source for bundles

Two approaches:

### Approach 1: Identify under-monetized components

Look for cheap, well-stocked components:
- Liquidation pallets often have decent items at \$1–\$3 each
- Discount retailers (TJ Maxx, Big Lots) for niche brand-name items
- Wholesale closeouts for end-of-line products

Bundle 3–4 of these into a themed kit and sell at 4–5× the component cost.

### Approach 2: Identify popular categories with weak bundles

Search Amazon for "[category] bundle" or "[category] gift set":
- Are there few competing bundles?
- Are existing bundles thin on quality?
- Could you assemble a better one?

If yes, sourcing for that bundle is your project.

## Setting up the bundle listing

Once you've designed your bundle:

### Step 1: Photograph the bundle

You need:
- Hero shot showing all components together
- Lifestyle shot showing the bundle in use
- Component close-ups (one per component)
- Packaging shot (the box/bag the bundle ships in)

5+ images minimum. White background hero, lifestyle for the rest.

### Step 2: Create the listing

In Seller Central → Inventory → Add Product → "I'm adding a product not sold on Amazon":

- **Product Name**: include "Bundle" or "Set" + the theme + key components
  - Example: "Coffee Lovers Gift Bundle: French Press + 12oz Premium Beans + Ceramic Mug"
- **Brand**: your brand (you can register a trademark for bundle-specific brands; otherwise use a generic "Bundle Co." style name)
- **Category**: pick the category of your most expensive component
- **Bullet points**: emphasize what's included + why each item matters
- **Description**: detailed, compelling, mention all components

### Step 3: Set quantity = 1

Each unit you list is one bundle (the components physically together). Don't list "5 units" expecting Amazon to bundle them — you ship physically-bundled units.

### Step 4: Pack and ship to FBA

Each bundle ships as a single unit. Tape components together (or polybag), apply your FNSKU, send to FBA.

## Common bundle mistakes

### Mistake 1: "Random box of stuff"

Three unrelated items thrown together. Amazon may merge it into a "lot" listing or remove it as not-a-bundle.

**Fix:** every bundle needs a unifying theme. "Stuff for moms" isn't a theme; "Mother's Day Spa Kit" is.

### Mistake 2: One component changes too often

Your bundle includes a popular brand item that goes in/out of stock at retail. You can't keep replenishing the bundle when one component disappears.

**Fix:** source bundles from stable, replenishable components. Avoid trendy brands.

### Mistake 3: Pricing too high

Bundles can't violate Amazon's value perception. If components cost \$5 each retail and you bundle 4 for \$60, customers see the bait.

**Fix:** target 40–60% above retail-component-sum. Above that, conversion craters.

### Mistake 4: Brand Registered components

Your bundle includes a Disney plush. Disney's brand team may complain even though you're listing as a bundle, not the individual component.

**Fix:** avoid Brand Registered components. Use generic or smaller-brand items.

### Mistake 5: Hijackers

Once your bundle proves profitable, others may copy your assembly + list against your ASIN. They become hijackers on your bundle listing.

**Fix:** Brand Registry your bundle brand (if scaling). Or stay below their radar by keeping volume modest.

## Bundle vs Multi-pack: choosing

If you're sourcing a multi-pack opportunity:
- **Multi-pack ASIN exists**: list against it, compete on Buy Box. Lower setup work, higher competition.
- **No multi-pack ASIN, single-pack ASIN exists**: create your own multi-pack listing. Medium setup, low competition initially.
- **Items don't naturally pack together**: design a bundle. Higher setup, lowest competition.

## Pricing strategy

For bundles, basic math:

```
Bundle price = (Sum of component costs) × Bundle multiplier

Bundle multiplier ranges:
- Functional bundle (everyday use):   1.6×–2.2×
- Gift bundle (themed):                2.0×–3.0×
- Specialty / hobby bundle:            2.5×–3.5×
- Holiday seasonal bundle:             3.0×–4.0× (during peak)
```

A coffee bundle with \$25 in components retails for \$50–\$80 depending on theme/season.

Test pricing for the first 30 days, then optimize:
- If conversion <2% on listing visitors: lower price 10%
- If conversion >5% with low offer count: raise price 10%
- If competing bundles exist: match closely or differentiate sharply

## Inventory management for bundles

Tricky compared to single-ASIN flips:
- Each component must be in stock
- Stockouts on one component = no more bundles can be assembled
- Storage at FBA: you ship pre-assembled bundles, so warehouse cost is per bundle (higher than per component)

Workflow:
1. Stockpile components at home
2. Assemble bundles in batches (10–20 at a time)
3. Ship to FBA when batch is built
4. Keep ~30 days of bundles at FBA, restock from your home stockpile

For replenishable components, set reorder triggers at "30 components remaining" so you don't run out.

## When bundles fail

Some bundle attempts fail. Reasons:
- ❌ Components changed shape/branding (e.g., supplier reformulated)
- ❌ Theme went out of style
- ❌ Cheap competitors offered same bundle for half the price
- ❌ Listing got merged into a generic listing by Amazon's catalog team

When a bundle dies:
- Sell through remaining inventory at clearance pricing
- Don't try to restock at the original margin
- Move on; bundle creation is a numbers game

## When bundles are gold

Some bundles run for 12+ months at high margins because:
- They're seasonal (Christmas decor, back-to-school, Mother's Day)
- They're niche enough that scale-resellers ignore them
- Components are stable (no supplier disruptions)
- The original creator is patient with low velocity (<10 sales/month) but high margin (50%+ ROI)

Bundle creators who win the long game treat each bundle as a small business. Don't need to win every shot — need enough hits to compound.
