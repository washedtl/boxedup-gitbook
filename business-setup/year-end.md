# Year-end & 1099-K

🟡 **Intermediate.** Year-end is when sloppy bookkeeping becomes expensive. Here's the checklist that prevents disaster.

## The 1099-K reality

Amazon issues you a 1099-K each January if your gross revenue from sales >\$600 (federal threshold as of 2024). Some states (MA, VA, etc.) have lower thresholds.

### What's on the 1099-K
- Gross sales (your "revenue")
- Number of transactions
- Per-month breakdown

### Critical detail
The 1099-K reports **GROSS revenue, not net income.** It does NOT subtract:
- Amazon fees
- Refunds
- Returns
- Your COGS
- Operating expenses

Many beginners panic seeing a 1099-K with "\$50,000 income" when they actually netted \$8,000. The IRS doesn't care about the gross — they want your accurate Schedule C with COGS + expenses subtracted.

## What you owe taxes on

Your taxable income for the year:
```
Gross sales (per 1099-K)
- Refunds & returns
- Amazon fees (referral, FBA, storage, etc.)
- COGS (cost of inventory sold using FIFO)
- Operating expenses
─────
Net business income (taxable)
```

If your books are clean, this calculation is straightforward. If they're not, you'll spend frantic weeks reconstructing in March/April.

## December close-out checklist

### Inventory reconciliation
- Run Amazon's "Inventory Reconciliation" report (full year)
- Match against your shipping records
- File any reimbursement claims for shortages (deadline: 18 months from event)
- Take physical count of inventory at home if applicable

### COGS verification
- For each SKU sold, verify cost basis in InventoryLab
- Update any missing or wrong costs
- Run "Profit by SKU" report to spot-check

### Operating expenses audit
- Pull all bank + credit card statements for the year
- Categorize any uncategorized transactions
- Verify all software, mileage, supplies tracked

### Mileage log finalization
If you drove for sourcing:
- Total miles for business purposes
- Standard mileage method: \$0.67/mile (2024 rate; check current year)
- Or actual expenses method (gas, maintenance, depreciation × business %)

### Final reimbursement claims
- Last chance to file for current-year claims before bookkeeping closes
- SellerBoard / similar should auto-flag missed cases

## Tax filing timeline

| Date | Action |
|---|---|
| **January 15** | Q4 estimated tax payment due |
| **January 31** | Amazon issues 1099-K |
| **February 1–28** | Compile all tax documents; send to CPA |
| **March 15** | LLC/S-corp filing deadline (without extension) |
| **April 15** | Personal/sole prop filing deadline; Q1 estimated tax due |
| **June 15** | Q2 estimated tax due |
| **September 15** | Q3 estimated tax due; LLC/S-corp extended deadline |
| **October 15** | Personal/sole prop extended deadline |

Get extensions automatically by filing Form 4868 (sole prop) or 7004 (LLC/corp). Extensions extend the FILING date, not the PAYMENT date — pay estimated tax by original deadline.

## DIY filing vs CPA

### DIY with TurboTax / similar
- Cost: \$50–\$200
- Works for: simple sole prop, <\$50K revenue, no S-corp election
- Pros: cheap, fast
- Cons: easy to miss deductions, limited tax-strategy advice

### CPA (Certified Public Accountant)
- Cost: \$300–\$3,000
- Works for: LLC, S-corp, multi-state, high revenue
- Pros: catches deductions, advises on tax strategy, peace of mind
- Cons: cost, time to find a good one

For most BoxedUp resellers past month 12: **CPA worth it.**

## The LLC tax filing

Single-member LLC: files as Schedule C on personal return (same as sole prop). LLC paperwork is for liability, not tax election.

Multi-member LLC: Form 1065 partnership return + K-1 to each partner.

S-corp election (LLC + Form 2553): Form 1120-S corporate return + W-2 for owner salary + K-1 for distributions.

## Estimated quarterly taxes

If your business is profitable, you owe estimated tax each quarter.

### Why
The IRS wants tax money throughout the year, not all at April. Failure to pay quarterly = underpayment penalty (small but cumulative).

### How much
Rule of thumb: **30% of net profit** set aside for taxes (covers federal + state + self-employment).

Quarterly estimate:
```
Estimated annual net profit × 30% = total tax owed
÷ 4 = quarterly payment
```

Example: \$60K projected annual profit → \$18K total tax → \$4,500/quarter.

### Where to pay
- Federal: IRS Direct Pay (irs.gov/payments)
- State: each state's Department of Revenue website
- Set up automatic ACH for ease

### Tracking
Make a "Tax savings" account (separate from operating bank). Move 30% of each Amazon disbursement into it. Quarterly, pay out from that account.

## S-corp owner salary requirement

If you elected S-corp, you must pay yourself a "reasonable salary" via formal payroll:

- Minimum salary: depends on industry, role, location
- Typical reseller salary: \$30K–\$60K
- Must run quarterly payroll (use Gusto, ADP, or your CPA)
- Pay payroll taxes on the salary portion
- Take additional money as "distributions" (no payroll tax)

**Don't mess up the salary.** IRS specifically targets S-corps with unrealistically low salaries to dodge SE tax. Penalties + reclassification can wipe years of S-corp savings.

## Common year-end mistakes

### Mistake 1: Forgetting reimbursements as income
You filed reimbursement claims; Amazon paid \$2,000. Forgot to record as income.

**Fix:** reimbursements ARE income (reseller-specific category). Audit your receivables in December.

### Mistake 2: Mismatched inventory + COGS
Books show 200 units sold, IL shows 180 units sold. \$1,000 discrepancy.

**Fix:** monthly reconciliation prevents year-end panic. Catch up if needed.

### Mistake 3: Personal expenses included
You forgot a "lunch out" meeting wasn't actually a business meeting. CPA caught it.

**Fix:** strict separation throughout the year.

### Mistake 4: Missed quarterly tax payment
You skipped Q3 because cash was tight. Penalty: ~\$300 + interest.

**Fix:** Tax-savings account discipline.

### Mistake 5: No CPA for first year
You DIY-filed, missed \$3K in deductions. CPA would have found them for \$500.

**Fix:** even if cost-conscious, hire CPA for first year. Learn their process, then DIY subsequent years if comfortable.

## Multi-state implications

Most BoxedUp arbitrage sellers don't have multi-state issues thanks to MPF. But:

### Sales tax: usually fine
Amazon collects + remits in 45+ states via MPF.

### Income tax: usually fine for low-revenue
- Single home state for income tax
- LLC files in home state only

### When multi-state income tax matters
- Multi-million-dollar operations
- Inventory stored in multiple states (FBA does this)
- Multiple physical operations

If you're <\$1M revenue: file in home state, ignore the rest.
If you're >\$1M revenue: hire e-commerce-specialist CPA who handles multi-state.

## Audit-prep mindset

Treat year-end like prep for a hypothetical audit:

- All transactions categorized + supported by receipts
- Mileage log has dates, destinations, purposes
- Inventory counts match Amazon + your records
- Bank statements reconciled
- Tax payments documented

If audited (rare for resellers <\$1M revenue, but possible):
- Provide records when requested
- IRS auditors are looking for fraud, not minor errors
- Clean books = quick audit + minimal damage
- Sloppy books = extended audit + back taxes + penalties

## Rolling over to next year

Once filed:

### Save records (7 years minimum)
- Tax return + supporting docs
- Bank statements
- Receipts
- Mileage logs

### Reset bookkeeping
- New fiscal year = new books
- Carry over inventory levels (non-zero remaining)
- Carry over balance sheet items (assets, liabilities)
- Income + expenses reset to zero

### Adjust strategy
- What worked tax-wise this year?
- Did you hit all deductions?
- Should you elect S-corp next year?
- Should you change entity structure?

A 30-minute review with CPA each January sets next year's strategy.

## Tax-planning beyond returns

For BoxedUp resellers past \$50K profit/year:

### Tax-advantaged retirement
- Solo 401(k) — up to \$66,000/year contribution
- SEP-IRA — up to 25% of net earnings
- Both reduce taxable income

### Equipment depreciation
- Section 179: deduct up to \$1.16M of business equipment in year of purchase
- Useful for: cars, computers, warehouse equipment

### Health insurance + HSA
- Self-employed health insurance fully deductible (above-the-line)
- HSA: triple-tax-advantaged account

### Hire family
- Spouse / kids working in business: reasonable wages = deductions
- Avoids self-employment tax on a portion

CPA helps you stack these legitimately. Beware schemes that sound too good to be true (they usually are).

## When you owe more than expected

If your year-end calculation shows you owe more than you've paid:

### Option 1: Pay the difference
By April 15. Penalty for underpayment is small.

### Option 2: Setup IRS payment plan
If you can't pay all at once, IRS offers installment agreements.
- Costs: setup fee + interest on balance
- Avoid: more aggressive collection

### Option 3: Reduce taxable income for next year
Aggressive (but legal) deductions:
- Open Solo 401(k) (deductible until April 15 for prior year — sometimes)
- Pre-pay annual subscriptions
- Buy needed business equipment

## When you're owed a refund

If you overpaid quarterly + have other deductions:

- Refund usually arrives 3–6 weeks after filing
- Use to: pay down credit cards, build inventory, reinvest in business
- Don't use to: extravagant personal spending (the refund isn't a bonus, it's your own money returned)
