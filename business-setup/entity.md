# LLC, sole prop, or wait?

🟢 → 🟡 — When to incorporate, what entity to choose, and why most BoxedUp resellers default to LLC.

> ⚠️ **Not legal or tax advice.** This page summarizes common reseller setups based on community experience. For your specific situation, consult a CPA or business attorney.

## The three options

| Entity | Setup cost | Annual cost | Liability protection | Tax treatment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Sole proprietorship** | \$0 | \$0 | None | Schedule C on personal taxes |
| **LLC** (single-member) | \$50–\$500 | \$0–\$800 | ✅ Yes | Schedule C (default) or S-corp election |
| **S-corp** | \$50–\$500 + accountant fees | \$500–\$3000 | ✅ Yes | Pass-through; quarterly payroll |

## When sole prop is fine

You're operating as a sole prop the moment you start selling — no paperwork required. Sole prop works if:

- You're testing the business (<\$5K/month in revenue)
- You haven't sourced any high-IP-risk inventory
- You have <\$10K of inventory at FBA at any point
- You're not commingling business + personal money yet

**The downside:** zero liability protection. If a customer sues over a defective product, your personal assets (house, savings) are on the line.

For most BoxedUp members past the first 90 days, sole prop is no longer adequate.

## When LLC makes sense

LLC is the default for serious resellers. You should incorporate as an LLC when:

- You're hitting \$5K+/month consistently
- You have >\$10K of inventory at FBA
- You sell anything category-risky (hazmat, food, beauty, electronics)
- You source private label / wholesale
- You're applying for gating that requires "business documentation"
- You want a business bank account separate from personal

LLC gives you:
- **Limited liability** — your personal assets are insulated from business lawsuits
- **EIN** — business tax ID needed for wholesale accounts, sales tax, etc.
- **Business bank account** — clean books, professional appearance
- **Credibility** — wholesale suppliers take "Acme Trading LLC" more seriously than "John Smith"

## When S-corp makes sense

S-corp is an LLC that elects to be taxed as a corporation. It's a tax optimization, not a different entity. Generally worth it when:

- Your **net business income** exceeds ~\$50K/year
- You can pay yourself a "reasonable salary" (~\$30K–\$60K typical for resellers)
- You're willing to run quarterly payroll
- You have a CPA who can handle S-corp tax filings

**The benefit:** you save on self-employment tax. As a sole prop / single-member LLC, you pay 15.3% SE tax on all profit. S-corp lets you split: salary (subject to SE tax) + distributions (no SE tax).

**The cost:** ~\$1500–\$3000/year in CPA + payroll fees. So the math works only if your savings exceed that.

Rough rule: **S-corp election pays off above \$60K/year net income.**

## State considerations

Where you form your LLC matters:

### Form in your home state (default, 90% of cases)
- Cheapest, simplest
- You don't have to register as a "foreign LLC" in your home state
- Annual report fees vary: \$0 (some states) to \$800 (California)

### Form in Delaware / Wyoming / Nevada (rare)
- Sometimes touted as "tax havens" or "privacy states"
- Reality: still pay tax in your home state if you live and work there
- Adds compliance costs (registered agent in the formation state + foreign-LLC registration in home state)
- Worth it only for very specific scenarios (multi-owner, raising capital, unique privacy needs)

For 99% of BoxedUp members: **form in your home state.** Don't overthink it.

## How to actually form an LLC

The process takes 1–4 weeks depending on state.

### DIY path (cheapest)

1. **Pick a name** — check availability on your state's Secretary of State website
2. **File Articles of Organization** — online via the SoS site, ~\$50–\$300 filing fee
3. **Get an EIN** — IRS.gov, free, takes 5 minutes
4. **Open a business bank account** — bring formation docs + EIN to any bank
5. **Get a sales tax permit** — your state's Department of Revenue, free or low cost
6. **Annual reports** — file as required (varies by state, usually annual)

Total cost: \$50–\$300 one-time + bank account opening fees.

### Online incorporation services

Sites like LegalZoom, Incfile, Bizee, Northwest Registered Agent will do it for you.

- **Incfile / Bizee** — \$0 + state fee (lowest cost), reasonable
- **Northwest Registered Agent** — ~\$39/year + state fee, good registered agent service
- **LegalZoom** — \$200+, expensive, lots of upsells (most can be skipped)

Pick a service if you'd rather not navigate the SoS website yourself. The LLC ends up identical.

### Hire a lawyer

Worth it if:
- You have business partners (operating agreement matters)
- You're raising outside capital
- You have unusual liability concerns
- You're forming in a non-home state for specific reasons

Cost: \$500–\$2000. Skip otherwise.

## What to do after formation

The day your LLC is approved:

### Week 1
- ✅ Get EIN from IRS
- ✅ Open business bank account (Chase, Mercury, Relay, BlueVine all work)
- ✅ Get a business credit card (American Express Blue Business Cash, Chase Ink)
- ✅ Update your Amazon seller account from "Individual" to "Business" (Settings → Account Info → Legal Entity)

### Week 2
- ✅ Apply for state sales tax permit
- ✅ Get a reseller certificate (some states)
- ✅ Set up bookkeeping software (QuickBooks, Wave, or InventoryLab + SellerBoard)
- ✅ Start funneling all business income/expenses through the business account

### Week 3+
- ✅ Update credit card on Amazon for the business card
- ✅ Apply for wholesale accounts using your new business credentials
- ✅ Apply for category ungating (now you have real documentation)

## Ongoing costs

After formation:

| Item | Annual cost |
|---|---|
| State annual report / franchise tax | \$0–\$800 |
| Registered agent (if separate) | \$0–\$150 |
| Business bank account | \$0–\$15/mo |
| Bookkeeping software | \$15–\$80/mo |
| Sales tax filing service (if needed) | \$0–\$50/mo |
| CPA (S-corp) | \$1500–\$3000 |

A typical sole-prop transitioning to LLC: ~\$200/year added compliance cost.

## Liability — how protected are you actually?

LLC liability protection is real but not absolute. To preserve it:

- **Don't commingle funds** — never use the business account for personal purchases
- **Sign contracts as the LLC**, not as yourself ("Acme Trading LLC, by John Smith, Member")
- **Keep records** of major business decisions (LLC operating agreement, even if single-member)
- **Maintain "corporate formalities"** — file annual reports, pay taxes on time
- **Don't personally guarantee debts** unless absolutely necessary

If you mix business and personal, courts can "pierce the corporate veil" and you lose the liability protection. Most LLC failures aren't structural — they're operational discipline failures.

## When to upgrade from LLC to LLC + S-corp

Once your net business income hits ~\$60K/year:

1. **Talk to a CPA** — they'll model whether S-corp makes sense for your specific income
2. **File Form 2553** — IRS election to be taxed as S-corp (must be filed by March 15 to apply for current year, or by 75 days after formation)
3. **Set up payroll** — Gusto, ADP, or your CPA's preferred service (~\$45–\$150/mo)
4. **Pay yourself a salary** — quarterly payroll runs
5. **Take owner distributions** — periodic transfers from business → personal, no payroll tax

Mistake to avoid: **electing S-corp too early.** Below \$60K/year, the payroll + CPA fees eat the SE tax savings.
