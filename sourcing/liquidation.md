# Liquidation & pallets

🟡 → 🔴 — Liquidation = buying pallets of returned, overstock, or shelf-pull inventory at deep discounts. High-risk, high-reward sourcing path. Most who try it lose money for the first 5 pallets.

## What liquidation actually is

Major retailers (Amazon itself, Walmart, Target, Macy's) end up with surplus inventory:
- Customer returns
- Overstock from canceled orders
- Damaged-but-functional units
- Shelf-pulls from store resets
- Discontinued SKUs

They sell this inventory in bulk to liquidators, who in turn sell to resellers via auction or wholesale.

You buy a pallet for \$500–\$5,000 and resell the contents at retail-ish prices.

## The economics

A typical pallet:
- Manifested retail value: \$5,000 (i.e., what the items would have retailed for new)
- Pallet auction price: \$500–\$1,500 (10–30% of manifest)
- Items sellable: 30–70% (some are damaged/unusable)
- Items sellable on Amazon: 20–50% (some can't be FBA'd)
- Net recovery: 1.5×–3× pallet cost if you're skilled

Returns:
- **Skilled liquidator:** 80–150% ROI per pallet (after labor, fees)
- **Average:** 20–60% ROI
- **Beginner:** 0–20% ROI or losses

## Where to source pallets

### Tier 1 (premium, vetted)
- **B-Stock Solutions** — auctions for major retailers (Macy's, Lowes, Home Depot, Costco)
- **BULQ** — Walmart returns, smaller cases
- **Liquidation.com** — broad inventory, multiple retailers
- **Direct Liquidation** — Walmart, Target, Costco
- **888 Lots** — case-quantity buys

### Tier 2 (regional)
- **Local liquidation auctions** — driving distance, in-person inspection
- **Estate sales** — household + retail mix
- **Storage unit auctions** — variable quality

### Tier 3 (avoid as a beginner)
- **Overseas wholesale lots** — counterfeit risk
- **Retail liquidation tours** — mostly trash
- **eBay "liquidation" listings** — usually picked-over

## What's in a typical pallet

Pallets are categorized:

### General Merchandise
- Mixed categories
- Higher variability in value
- Hardest to predict ROI

### Electronics
- High-margin but high-counterfeit risk
- Many returns are functional but missing accessories
- Skilled liquidators love these; beginners get burned

### Apparel
- Lower per-unit value
- Most categories require gating
- Labor-intensive (many small items to process)

### Home goods / Kitchen
- Steady mid-margin
- Lower counterfeit risk
- Beginner-friendly

### Toys
- Q4 spike pattern
- Many returns are damaged packaging only
- Brand IP risk (avoid Tier 1 brands)

### Health & Beauty
- Often expired or near-expired
- Gating required
- Skip until you have category ungating

## Manifest reading

A "manifest" is the spreadsheet listing what's on the pallet. Reading it is the skill that separates winners from losers.

A manifest line:
```
Item: KitchenAid Stand Mixer KSM150PSER
Condition: Customer Return
Qty: 2
Retail value: $429.99 each
```

What you actually get:
- Maybe both work perfectly
- Maybe one works, one is broken
- Maybe both are missing accessories
- Maybe the box is destroyed
- Maybe it's not even a stand mixer (manifest errors are common)

**Before buying:**
1. **Check the manifest carefully** — look for high-value items you can sell
2. **Discount expected recovery** — assume 50% of manifest value as realistic max recovery
3. **Compare to pallet price** — is the math reasonable?

A manifest at 30% of retail value = \$5K manifest for \$1.5K pallet. If you recover 60% = \$3K. ROI = (3000 - 1500) / 1500 = 100%. Not great after labor, but workable.

A manifest at 10% = \$5K manifest for \$500 pallet. If you recover 60% = \$3K. ROI = 500%. Much better.

## Receiving a pallet

When the pallet arrives:

### Day 1: Triage
1. Photograph the pallet before opening (claims defense if anything's missing)
2. Check the manifest count against actual count
3. Note major discrepancies (missing items)
4. File a claim with the auction site if shortage > 5%

### Days 2–5: Inspect each item
1. Plug in / power on each electronic
2. Check apparel for damage / used wear
3. Check home goods for completeness (missing parts kill resale)
4. Sort into 3 piles:
   - **Resellable as new** (in original packaging, undamaged)
   - **Resellable as used** (functional but opened/used)
   - **Trash** (broken, missing, unsellable)

### Days 6–10: List + ship
1. Resellable as new → list on Amazon at competitive price → FBA
2. Resellable as used → eBay, Mercari, Facebook Marketplace, or Amazon "Used"
3. Trash → recycle / dispose

## Liquidation pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Counterfeit risk

Counterfeits show up in liquidation pallets. If you list a counterfeit on Amazon, you risk inauthentic complaints + suspension.

**Mitigation:**
- Only liquidate pallets from major retailers (Macy's, Walmart, Target) — not "general merchandise" lots from unknown sources
- Inspect every electronic for serial numbers + verify with the brand
- For high-counterfeit items (Apple, designer goods): skip entirely

### Pitfall 2: Missing parts

A KitchenAid mixer without the bowl is worthless on Amazon's "new" listing. Common with returns.

**Mitigation:** open every box, verify all components, only list complete units.

### Pitfall 3: Damaged units sold as new

You list a returned but functional unit as "new" because the manifest said new. It's actually been used. Customer complains "Used as new."

**Mitigation:** be conservative. Anything that's been opened or shows wear → list as "Used - Like New" not "New."

### Pitfall 4: Cumulative IP risk

Pallets often include a few high-value brand items (Stanley, Yeti, Apple). Liquidating and listing them creates IP claim risk.

**Mitigation:** sell branded items off-Amazon (eBay, Facebook Marketplace) where IP enforcement is lower.

### Pitfall 5: Storage explosion

You bought 3 pallets and now your garage is unusable. Selling-through takes 3–6 months. By month 2, you've lost track of inventory.

**Mitigation:** ONE pallet at a time until you've sold through it. Discipline > volume.

## When liquidation works

Liquidation pays off in specific conditions:

- ✅ You have garage / storage space
- ✅ You're willing to spend 8–20 hours processing per pallet
- ✅ You're patient with cash flow (3–6 months sell-through)
- ✅ You have multiple sales channels (Amazon + eBay + Mercari + Facebook)
- ✅ You're not your only customer service rep (some returns get complicated)

If those don't match: **stick with RA + OA + wholesale.** Liquidation isn't required for any reseller success.

## Liquidation vs other sources

When to pick liquidation:

| Goal | Best path |
|---|---|
| Highest velocity | Wholesale |
| Highest margin per unit | RA / OA on hot SKUs |
| Highest variety | Liquidation |
| Lowest time investment | Wholesale (after relationships built) |
| Best for beginners | RA → OA |
| Best for "treasure hunters" | Liquidation |

## Categories to focus on as a liquidation beginner

If you're starting:
- ✅ **Home goods** — kitchen, decor, small appliances
- ✅ **Sporting goods** — niche, lower IP risk
- ✅ **Tools** — Lowes/HD pallets, durable
- ❌ **Apparel** — labor-heavy, low margin, gating
- ❌ **Electronics** — high IP risk, technical inspection needed
- ❌ **Beauty/Health** — gated, expiration risk

## Pallet flipping (selling pallets to others)

Some sourcers buy pallets, partial-list the gems on Amazon, and **resell the rest as smaller "subpallets"** to other resellers via Facebook groups.

Math: pay \$1,200 for a pallet, list \$800 worth of items on Amazon, sell the remaining \$2,000 of inventory as "as-is" subpallets for \$700. Net profit before sales: \$300 + whatever you sell on Amazon.

This is its own niche. Skip until you've moved 10+ pallets through Amazon directly.

## Calculating real ROI on liquidation

Be brutally honest:

```
Pallet cost:           $1,500
Shipping (if any):     +$200
Time to process:       20 hours @ $25/hr = $500
Total invested:        $2,200

Amazon-listed inventory:    $3,500
Amazon fees (35%):          -$1,225
Amazon net:                 $2,275

eBay-listed inventory:      $400
Trash:                      $0

Total recovery:             $2,675
Net profit:                 $475
ROI:                        21.6%
```

That's a "good" pallet for a beginner. After 5–10 pallets, you'll get faster and more selective, pushing ROI above 50%.
