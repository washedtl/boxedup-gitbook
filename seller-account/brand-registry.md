# Brand Registry (when it matters)

🟡 **Intermediate.** Brand Registry is Amazon's program for brand owners. If you're doing arbitrage, you don't need it — but understanding what it does helps you avoid IP traps. If you're starting a private label, it's mandatory.

## What Brand Registry actually is

Amazon Brand Registry gives the owner of a registered trademark:
- **Listing control** — they own the listing details (title, images, description, A+ content)
- **IP enforcement** — fast-track removal of unauthorized sellers
- **Tools** — Brand Analytics, Brand Stories, Sponsored Brand ads, Vine
- **Visibility** — branded storefronts, video content, premium placements

It's free for the brand owner — but requires a registered USPTO trademark (or equivalent in other countries).

## Who needs it

### You ARE running private label
Brand Registry is essential. Without it:
- Anyone can hijack your listing
- You can't run Sponsored Brand ads
- You can't use A+ content
- You're locked out of Amazon's brand-protection tools

Get USPTO trademark + Brand Registry within 90 days of launching.

### You're doing arbitrage
You don't need Brand Registry for your own brand. But you should know which brands you're selling against ARE in Brand Registry, because:
- Brand Registered = aggressive IP enforcement
- Brand Registered + Storefront = brand controls listing tightly
- Brand Registered without authorization = high suspension risk for you

### You're doing wholesale
Generally don't need it for your wholesale business. But if you've negotiated exclusive distribution or private-label-style branding with a supplier, you might.

## How to spot Brand Registered listings

When you scan an ASIN, three signals indicate Brand Registry:

### Signal 1: The Storefront badge
Top-right of listing page shows "Visit the [Brand] Store." Click it → you'll see a custom-branded storefront with multiple pages, lifestyle imagery, video.

### Signal 2: A+ content
Below the buy box, the description includes:
- Module-style image+text blocks
- Side-by-side product comparison charts
- High-res lifestyle photography
- "About this brand" sections

A+ content requires Brand Registry (and Vendor Central or Seller Central with Brand Registry).

### Signal 3: Sponsored Brand ads
Search for the brand on Amazon. If you see a top banner ad with the brand logo + "Sponsored," they're running Sponsored Brand campaigns — which require Brand Registry.

If a listing has all three signals, the brand owner is **fully invested in their Amazon presence** — meaning they care a lot about who sells on the listing, and they have the tools to enforce it.

## Risk tiers for arbitrage sellers

| Tier | Description | Examples | Source approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1** | Brand Registered + aggressive enforcement | Disney, Nintendo, Apple, LEGO, Stanley | Don't source unless you have official wholesale invoice |
| **Tier 2** | Brand Registered, occasional enforcement | Sony, Bose, Yeti, Hydro Flask | Source carefully, save receipts, keep volume modest |
| **Tier 3** | Brand Registered, lax enforcement | Most CPG, mid-tier toys, generic categories | Source freely with normal precautions |
| **Tier 4** | Not Brand Registered | Generic products, smaller brands, no-name | Lowest IP risk, easiest sourcing |

The further down the list, the more comfortable you should be sourcing.

## How to register your own brand

If you're going private label or own a brand:

### Step 1: Get a USPTO trademark

- Go to **uspto.gov**
- Search the database to ensure your brand isn't taken
- File a trademark application (~\$250–\$350 in government fees per class)
- Wait 8–12 months for approval

USPTO trademarks come in **classes** — you trademark in the specific category you sell in (e.g., Class 28 = Toys & Games). You can register in multiple classes if you sell across categories.

**Pro tip:** for fastest Brand Registry, file under "Standard Character Mark" (text only, no logo). Logo trademarks take longer and are more expensive to defend.

### Step 2: Apply to Brand Registry

- Brand Registry portal → Apply
- Submit your USPTO serial number (you don't need full approval, just a filed application that's reached a certain stage — depends on Amazon's current rules)
- Provide your company info, brand name, primary product image
- Wait 1–4 weeks for approval

### Step 3: Use the tools

After approval:
- **Listing control** — claim control of your ASINs
- **A+ content** — build module-rich descriptions
- **Brand Analytics** — see search-term data, competitor performance
- **Sponsored Brand ads** — run banner ads in search results
- **Vine** — get free reviews from incentivized reviewers
- **Transparency** — anti-counterfeit codes (advanced; ~\$0.01–\$0.05/code)

## Transparency Program

For brand owners who want bulletproof anti-counterfeit:
- Each unit you sell gets a unique scannable code
- Customers (and Amazon) scan to verify authenticity
- Counterfeit reports are 1-click filings with code evidence
- Amazon won't allow listings without valid codes

Costs: \$0.01–\$0.05 per code + manufacturer integration. Worth it for any brand >\$50K/month and high-counterfeit categories (apparel, accessories, supplements).

## What "brand abuse" looks like

If you're a brand owner, common abuses to watch for:

### Listing hijacking
Unauthorized seller adds their offer to your Brand Registered listing.
- **Fix:** Brand Registry portal → Report a violation → Suspected counterfeit / unauthorized seller

### Counterfeit
Listings using your trademark for fake products.
- **Fix:** Test buy → photograph → file with Brand Registry; Amazon usually removes within 24–72 hours

### Listing manipulation
Someone changes your title/images via "Suggest changes."
- **Fix:** Brand Registry portal → Report → Edit your own listings (you control them as registered brand)

### Sponsored ads against your brand
Competitor bids on your brand name.
- **Note:** This is legal in Amazon's ad system. You can't block it. Only mitigation: defend your brand-name searches with your own ads.

## What "brand defense" looks like

Five tools brand owners use to keep listings clean:

1. **Project Zero** (large brands only) — directly remove counterfeit listings without filing
2. **Transparency Program** — anti-counterfeit codes
3. **Brand Registry filing** — manual reports of violations
4. **Test buys** — Amazon's brand-protection team buys suspicious offers and tests for authenticity
5. **Litigation** — for repeat counterfeiters

Most BoxedUp resellers will never be on the receiving end of #4 or #5 — those are for confirmed counterfeiters. But staying off Tier 1 brands (above) keeps you out of the queue.

## Vendor Central vs Seller Central with Brand Registry

A subtle distinction:

| | Vendor Central | Seller Central + Brand Registry |
|---|---|---|
| Who you sell to | Amazon (B2B) | Customers (D2C) |
| Pricing control | Amazon sets retail | You set retail |
| Inventory risk | Amazon owns inventory | You own inventory |
| Margin | Lower (wholesale margin) | Higher (retail margin) |
| Invitation | Invite-only by Amazon | Self-serve enroll |

99% of BoxedUp brand owners are Seller Central + Brand Registry. Vendor Central is mostly for established brands Amazon's vendor managers reach out to.

## A note on "borrowed" Brand Registry

Some sellers buy/lease access to someone else's Brand Registry to get the benefits. **Don't.**

- It violates Amazon's TOS
- Amazon's anti-fraud tools detect ownership transfers
- Result: account suspension for both parties

If you want Brand Registry benefits, register your own brand. Period.
